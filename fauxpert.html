Constructing the Fauxpert

">
Hannah Arendt from The Human Condition

Expertise is a foundational requirement for academic and professional research publications. The SPN hires experts and professionals with advanced degrees to write articles, commentaries, and misleading research reports for its websites. When University faculty and professional experts use their credentials to write and publish disinformation they are what I term fauxperts. Fauxperts sell their credibility to disinformation networks like the SPN to manufacture an illusion of trustworthiness. Blending opinion and disinformation together allows fauxperts to intimidate less educated and inexperienced audiences who are hesistant to question experts out of fear of being wrong or embarrassed. Most people do not willingly admit that they are ignorant on a topic. Generating and promoting homegrown evidence and endorsing misleading claims for SPN affiliates is the fauxpert's primary contribution to the disinformation network. They often times use their authority and reputation to promote and advance arguments that fall well outside their speciality. Fauxperts also adopt a variety of genres typically reserved for delivering highly credible and respected research to academic and professional communities. Designing fake scholarship and research to support conspiracy theories and unsubstantiated claims can slow down an audience's ability to verify information quickly. Most of the content fauxperts produce for the SPN is well outside their field of expertise; it's simply the expert's opinion on an issue outside their field that is couched as expertise. University faculty and professionals are more than aware of the unethicality of selling their credentials to validate arguments that are beyond the scope of their expertise. For example, in “When Red State Governors Act Like Blue,” an article published by the Indiana Policy Review , Dr. Richard Moss, a surgeon, makes outlandish claims about Covid.

In this example, Moss advances a series of racist stereotypes, tropes, and bigoted opinions for a pseudo journal. The quality of research and the standards and methodologies required for argumentation within educated communities is not the same quality we find in SPN journals, but the presence of a fauxpert in a fake journal article gives its claims authority. Even though the journal's tone and language do not meet the expectations and standards for traditional academic and professional publications, most of the SPN's targeted audiences won't recognize the difference between a real and fake journal article. When academic faculty and professional researchers assist in designing disinformation (with specific genres of authority), they create additional layers of disinformation that are extremely difficult for users to identify and pull apart. Furthermore, certain genres have structures and forms that are highly epistemic, that map out knowledge making in a field, regardless of the content. Fauxperts rely on these types of genres to endorse claims that enhance the SPN's agenda. As a mouthpiece for the SPN, the fauxpert is someone who filters and sanitizes disinformation. The Indiana Policy Review hired Dr. Moss to use his medical background to push false claims about Covid without providing any medical evidence. The SPN is appropriating Moss's credentials to spread disinformation about Covid. Using medical credentials to disguise an opinion as sound medical advice in a fake research journal is unethical and perilous. Calling the Indiana Policy Review a journal is also highly misleading. For example, in another article titled “Armageddon for Red China” Dr. Moss perpetuates a conspiracy theory about China making Covid in a lab and spreading it around the world on purpose. He refers to Covid as the Chinese Virus, further amplifying racist and derogatory conspiracy theories about Covid that are now being promoted on the White House website, expanding the network's message as a deep story. The CDC has continually reiterated the dangers of using hydroxychloroquine to treat Covid, but Musk and the Trump administration are gutting the CDC and replacing health communication with disinformation. The SPN has become a mouthpiece and lobbyist for the current administration's agenda.

The Pioneer Institute (PI) provides a list of fauxperts on its website to construct the illusion that the PI consults with a team of objective academic and professional researchers. Below is a list of experts who supposedly contribute to the PI, but I was unable to find any contributions to the PI from anyone on the list in Figure 2. Providing names of experts on the website signals the presence of expertise at the PI and it keeps most users from checking how these experts contribute to the PI.

The Buckeye Institute (BI) also hires experts with advanced degrees to produce fake economic research. Having a fauxpert write a fake journal article or research report forms several layers of distortion users can't always see. Fauxperts like Andrew Kidd help construct an image of credibility for the SPN that doesn't really exist. The BI is not an academic institution or university, or a research institute like the Jet Propulsion Lab. It is a pseudo-research institution, pseudo-policy forum, and pseudo-foundation that hires well-educated people like Kidd to generate and endorse disinformation. The BI is also appropriating itself with Ohio's flagship university, Ohio State University, to manufacture an association with a research institution that doesn't exist. These layers of illusion impair audiences' ability to discern fact from fiction. When audiences visit SPN websites and see that the writers and researchers have advanced degrees, or that a writer is affiliated with a prestigious research university like Notre Dame, they are likely to relax their critical faculties and habits of mind and become more willing to trust that source. Fauxperts deactivate someone's critical habits of mind and make them overlook problematic content because it is from an expert, but expertise is specific and not general.

In an article in the Indiana Policy Review Journal , John F. Gaski, a business professor from Notre Dame, argues that Trump's claims about the effectiveness of hydroxychloroquine were scientifically valid and posed no danger to the public, even though this professor's Ph.D. is in business and not virology. Audiences need to check how an expert's specialization aligns with the arguments and claims they make and determine if their expertise extends beyond a disciplinary boundary. Gaski's credentials and affiliation with a prestigious unviersity enhances baseless and perilous claims about an ineffective and dangerous treatment for Covid. Dr. Gaski's rant about hydroxychloroquine lacks evidentiary support and recklessly advances a dangerous conspiracy theory about the effectiveness of a drug that the vast majority of the medical community definitively argues is deadly.

Figure 2: Gaski as Fauxpert. Accessed 6-18-2020.

Furthermore, many people who read Graski's article will see his credentials, trust his ethos, and relax their critical habits of mind. Several users will never even realize his expertise is in business and not medicine. Reading a journal article comes with an assumption that the author, research, and content have been verified, so a fake journal article by a fauxpert creates two additional layers of disinformation that have to be peeled back and exposed. Many readers rely on traditional markers of credibility like the author's education and expertise to determine the honesty of a piece of information and to make decisions about its value. Someone reading this journal article, written by an expert from a prestigious universisty, might overlook Graski's baseless claims on issues outside of his expertise. It's troubling that a business professor from the University of Notre Dame is amplifying a conspiracy theory about the effectiveness of hydroxychloroquine for a politicized disinformation network.

In the era of disinformation, everyone needs to be able to verify expertise in digital sources. Fauxperts like Gaski, Moss, and Kidd write research documents on issues outside of their expertise and pass them off as credible scholarship and research on SPN websites. Accounting for how expertise is established in digital writing environments is an important technical literacy for recognizing disinformation. Hiring people with advanced degrees to produce pseudo-scholarship and faux-academic research and spread it across a disinformation network is eroding democracy and polluting the media ecosystem. Every SPN website provides a list of fauxperts who have earned PhD's, graduate degrees, or MD's, and who sell their expertise to a disinformation network. Knowing how to verify expertise in digital writing environments, seeing how expertise bolsters the credibility of a disinformation network, is a form of crap detection. To move forward and out of a disinformation era, the people will need to regain control of local and national media outlets and rebuild a healthy information ecosystem: conclusion.
