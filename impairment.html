Hannah Arendt from The Human Condition

This section examines how the SPN uses digital disinformation to impair users and it notes how to identify disinformation in digital writing environments. Seeing examples of what networked disinformation looks like and how it does what it does is important for expanding one's critical, digital literacies. A disinformation network's main goal is to impair people from making a sound decision or thinking for themselves. Again, many of the webpages I examined for this project were taken down or removed after they served their purpose. A large amount of disinformation I encountered on these websites appeared for a short period of time in an attempt to alter public opinion for an upcoming election or local decision. The most common disinformation tactics for impairing audiences that I found were:

Designing Impairment

No References

The SPN does not provide references or links to locate sources in its publications.

Ambiguous Titles

The SPN labels its affiliates as foundations, institutes, or policy groups,

503C NonProfit Status

The SPN uses nonprofit status to hide its shady behavior.

Citing Homegrown Research

The SPN creates and cites its own research.

Circulating Acontexual Data

The SPN circulates large data sets that have no context.

Imitating Professionial Genres

The SPN mimicks professional and academic genres to give the illusion of ethos.

In this section, I focus on examining the first three impairment strategies. Each of the last three strategies has its own page of analysis. The 503(c)(3) nonprofit status forms an initial layer, a first-layer, of manufactured credibility and it helps the SPN hide its biased and political intentions. A nonprofit status signals to audiences that the information on SPN websites is credible because it's not for profit, but correlating a nonprofit to honesty and integrity has been untrue for decades. Nonprofit organizations have implemented a range of donation systems to collect large amounts of money to produce disinformation and avoid paying taxes. Moreover, labeling SPN affiliates as foundations, institutes, or policy groups manufactures another layer of credibility that keeps audiences from questioning the sincerity of the content on the websites. These terms are associated with a culture and tradition of officiality and authority that leads people away from making assumptions about what these organizations actually do. When people believe information comes from a nonprofit foundation or a credible organization, they are more likley to relax their critical habits of mind. A nonprofit foundation or institute can signal to audiences that the information they produce and share are not politically or financially motivated and do not promote extreme political and social bias. However, people are abusing nonprofit status to manipulate audiences. No one should be able to use a nonprofit status to prevent or limit people from noticing disinformation. Everyone needs to approach a nonprofit organization with some skepticism before learning more about its motives. The SPN also uses fake references to disinform audiences.

The SPN deliberately complicates its audiences' ability to verify sources in its publications. Obfuscating source material forces audiences to either trust the content or spend extra time and energy verifying it, which, in some circumstances, can take hours, days, or even weeks to complete. In other words, it's a designed waste of time. I spent just over 100 hours verifiying sources on SPN websites for this project. Making audiences locate where data in a chart came from or who wrote a source is a disinformation tactic students must learn to recognize. It's unethical to ask audiences to verify the existence of sources in a digital writing enviornment. One of the first examples of impairment I provide comes from an article on the Georgia Public Policy Committee's (GPCC) website that references an unsourced graph to inaccurately correlate the presence and absence of polar bears with climate change. Harold Brown references several sources and even provides quotes in the article, but he does not provide any links or citations for readers to verify those sources. The graph states “Various Sources” at the top of the page but the article does not provide any information about those sources or identify any of the sources referenced in the article. It also fails to provide a bibliography or works cited page so users can easily locate and verify sources. Failing to provide an efficient way to locate sources is a common characteristic of disinformation. Below is a screenshot of and link to the graph and article:

Figure 1: GPPF Website. Problematic Evidence. 6-6-2020.

The article mentions a report from the First International Meeting on the Polar Bear in 1967 in Alaska, but there is no way to verify this source, and the report is from 1967. I located the report online after searching for it on Google. The report is over 84 pages and Brown does not provide page numbers in the source reference, so I was unable to locate the quote Brown referenced to the report in the article. Brown blends together confusing data and outdated research and then fails to provide links and references to slow down the audiences ability to verify claims. It would take someone several hours to verify this source, which is an overwhelming time commitment. This is an indication that Brown is using citations and resources to disinform readers. Audiences should be concerned with references to outdated articles and a lack of an efficient method to verify sources. Taking the time to provide source verification in digital writing environments is a deliberate attempt to prevent disinformation.

Brown also references a research article on tropical atolls to inaccurately correlate an increase in island size with a lack of global warming. He uses the article to insist that if global warming were occurring, islands would be decreasing in size; however, Brown must not have read the article he cited in his report because it very clearly accounts for other physical transformations occurring on the island as a result of global warming. The article that Brown relies on to make his argument actually concludes that the measurement for erosion on some parts of the island can “be offset by accretion on parts of the coastline” (Webb & Kench, 2010), and that accretion can give the illusion that erosion is minimal when, in fact, it is significant but less noticeable, considering the total size of the island. Brown's reference to a 43% increase in island formation is taken from the article's abstract on the first page. Had he read the entire article, he would have found that "assessments of island change must be evaluated at the 'whole island' scale involving analysis of potential change in the entire island perimeter” ( (Webb & Kench, 2010) and not just at one part of the island. Coastal erosion is not representative of the whole island's total erosion. Most people don't have time to verify this information. Using sea level data on a select set of locations to falsely represent global sea level rising is disinformation. Referencing 14 specific locations that have unique land formations that experience a variety of effects from global warming, locations that are very different from other land formations, and then using those 14 locations to represent global warming erosion is misleading.

It took me over five hours to read this article and vet Brown's article, sources, and claims. Brown cites scientific jargon from a journal article to give the illusion that he synthesized research. Checking the references in an opaque digital source will often result in locating disinformation. Audiences need to learn how disinformation networks cite credible sources that do not have a succinct connection to the argument. The presence of a credible reference in a source is not enough to determine the relevancy of the source. The interjection of source must be fair, honest, and directly connected to the argument. Teaching students how to critique source interjection in digital writing environments is important for discovering disinformation and developing crap detection. Audiences should be suspicious of references that fail to provide the title or the author of the source, the title of the publication outlet, or a reference or works cited page to locate sources quickly. Slowing down the ability to verify information requires very little effort to design and execute in digital writing environments. In print publications, there are more opportunities to review and prevent content from publication. Impairing someone's ability to verify digital sources erodes democracy at an unprecedented rate. Democratic communication systems require efficient methods and practices for validating information in a timely manner. The Independence Institute is another SPN affiliate that fails to provide verifiable evidence for its claims in a published report on arming teachers.

More examples of the SPN using disinformation to impair users.

The Goldwater Institute (GI), out of Arizona, writes and publishes hundreds of reports that promote SPN target issues. In the "Rage against Chloroquine," Mark Flatten cites medical evidence from the NY Post and references several questionable websites to support his claims. He also fails to cite any medical scholarship or peer-reviewed research on hydroxychloroquine. Flatten's article asserts that the medical community has no idea how hydroxychloroquine affects humans or how effective the drug is, or can be, to treat Covid; however, the medical community has consistently asked the general public not to use hydroxychloroquine to treat Covid because it is dangerous.

All of the sources referenced in Flatten's article are questionable and require verification. The time required to verify these sources is overwhelming. I spent hours searching for sources. Many users will likely trust these references because they come from fauxperts, and they're woven into professional and academic genres that reduce audiences' desire to verify them. Even though doctors have a long, historical understanding of hydroxychloroquine and its effects on humans, Flatten argues that hydroxychloroquine is the “most promising” drug for treating Covid but he does not provide evidence to support that claim.

This article's disinformation on hydroxychloroquine echoes existing narratives about the effectiveness of the drug that many politicians pushed in press conferences in 2020 at the height of Covid. The trusted medical community and the CDC, before the current Trump administration took it over, continually noted the dangers of using the drug to treat Covid. The webpage has been removed. The CDC also released a report that claimed hydroxychloroquine is not only ineffective for treating Covid but it is lethal to humans. In 2025, the CDC was defunded and thousands of employees were fired by Elon Musk and DOGE in a massive government purge. Covid Disinformation

In "Busting Myths", the author asserts that all teachers carry guns outside of school, disarm themselves every day for work, and then immediately rearm themselves when they get to the car after work, inferring that teachers will not mind carrying a gun in the classroom and protecting students. But there is no evidence to support this claim. The report also predicts that an overwhelming number of teachers would be willing to volunteer to carry a gun in the classroom, but it does not offer any evidence to support that claim.

Research on arming teachers maintains that a majority of national and state school boards oppose arming teachers, and many national and state surveys that ask teachers if they would carry a gun on campus have definitively shown that teachers emphatically do not want to carry a gun on campus or in the classroom. A California State University professor conducted a study on arming teachers in schools and found that less than 15% of teachers even owned a gun, and that over 90% of teachers did not want to carry a gun in the classroom. The article on busting myths is actually creating one. Audiences should be aware of sources without references and citations in digital writing environments. This type of distortion of sources is common on SPN websites. There are hundreds of documents on SPN websites that disseminate disinformation in this same way.

The Buckeye Institute (BI) wrote a policy memo to encourage using telehealth because it benefits people who have low mobility and live far from medical care. However, the references in the memo that support these claims are disconnected. For example, the reference about people with “low-mobility” benefiting from telehealth specifically addresses homebound patients with depression who need psychotherapy (Buckeye Institute). The reference supporting the claim that people who “live far” away from medical care benefit from telehealth is from an article about specialized pediatric care in rural communities and it fails to address the benefits of telehealth more broadly. The title of one of the articles referenced in the memo is “Six-Month Postintervention Depression and Disability Outcomes of In-Home Telehealth Problem-Solving Therapy for Depressed, Low-Income Homebound Older Adults,” and the other article is from a pediatric's journal. Telehealth for children with special needs who live in rural areas is not a typical patient population, nor does it represent the total population or reflect how children with special needs as a patient population would typically use telehealth.

Homebound depressed patients who need psychotherapy are also not a representation of general patient care. Having access to healthcare has been a problem for these specific patients before and after Covid, so using these sources to address telehealth in relation to the pandemic and this specific patient population is misleading. Misconnecting and misrepresenting source material is a disinformation tactic designed to impair one's critical thinking.

The artificial connectivity between the references and the claims in a report with graphs and statistics manifests the illusion of credibility. The structures of a genre like a report evoke a desire to recognize those structures. "Genres have this generative power because they maintain the desires that elicit their use—socially sanctioned motives for 'appropriately' recognizing and behaving within certain recurring situations" (Bawarshi, p. 96). The patterns of genres are alluring because they call us to join them, to engage and complete their meaning.

There are several questions left unanswered in this survey that audiences need to learn how to ask. The marketing firm polled 500 people, but who were these people? The article claims that voters were polled, but there are no references or summaries of the demographics of the respondents for the survey or where and when the poll was conducted. The article states that the firm administered the poll across the state, but it does not provide the name of any of the cities and counties where the polling took place. When users click on the link for the summary of the poll results, they are taken to the results of an unrelated poll on Pennsylvanians' top priorities. If someone fails to critically examine these polls and links, they will miss the sleight of hand. The CWF relies on the results of another unrelated poll to further suggest that residents approve of opening the state as soon as possible.

This poll also states, ironically, that healthcare is a top priority for voters and that opening the economy too early during a pandemic would threaten the community's health. The CWF is trying to use data from two different, unrelated polls to support a claim that neither poll can support on its own. Pam Lewson, from the Washington Policy Center (WPC), proclaimed that her research found that "the application of Chlorpyrifos” as a pesticide in Washington “is extremely safe" and has been used in the state safely for years, but she fails to cite any research to support this claim.

Lewson's unsupported claims about the safety of Chlorpyrifos are dangerous. A basic search on chlorpyrifos reveals the significant dangers of this pesticide and the many attempts to ban it because of its direct links to cancer and other health concerns in humans. The Baltimore Sun reported that “The Children's Environmental Health and Protection Advisory Council firmly supports legislation in the 2020 Maryland General Assembly that would ban the toxic pesticide chlorpyrifos.” The Baltimore Sun insisted that “A wealth of science demonstrates adverse health effects of chlorpyrifos exposure to developing fetuses, infants, children and pregnant women.” The Oregon Attorney General also reported that “Chlorpyrifos has been well-documented to harm proper development and functioning of the central nervous system and brain.” The evidence on the dangers of chlorpyrifos is significant. In a Pediatrics article, several doctors found that children exposed to chlorpyrifos “scored lower on the Bayley Psychomotor Development Index” and “the Bayley Mental Development Index at 3 years of age compared with those with lower levels of exposure” (Raugh et al., 2006). Doctors also found that children exposed to higher levels of chlorpyrifos were “more likely to experience Psychomotor Development Index and Mental Development Index delays, attention problems, attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder problems, and pervasive developmental disorder problems at 3 years of age” (Raugh et al., 2006). The SPN and Lewson are creating, publishing, and circulating disinformation that is highly dangerous to the general public's health. Even the company that makes chlorpyrifos, Corteva, decided to halt production in 2019 because of the dangers the chemical posed to the public, yet it is still legal to use despite these health ramifications.

The EPA banned the use of chlorpyrifos in building construction and attempted to ban its use in the US in 2016, but the Trump administration halted the ban. The EPA also attempted to ban the use of chlorpyrifos on crops, but Scott Pruitt, the EPA Director at the time of this research, wrote an order that postponed a final decision on chlorpyrifos until the end of 2022. The current administration in 2025 is also limiting the EPA's ability to regulate chemicals and products and defunding federal programs that generate health and science data. The US is currently experiencing a more formal dismantling of health expertise across federal and state institutions. The Trump administration is cutting NIH funding for science and health research and propping and sanitizing disinformation about vaccines, fluoride, and autism. RFK jr., a conspiracy theorist, former heroin addict, eugenicist, and anti vaxxer who does not have a medical degree or experience, has purged several respected health experts from the Health and Human Services department and replaced them with supporters of pseudo science.

Impairment is an extremely effective and foundational disinformation tactic. If misleading information can appear authentic and credible, users might trust the information. If the content imitates or mimics professional and academic genres, some users may not feel compelled enough to verify content and references. Slowing someone's verification process down or eliminating it all together is an effective disinformation tactic because it does not require a monumental effort to achieve. Ong et al. (2018) affirmed that many times “Trust overrides deceptive intent” (p. 59), and if users trust the SPN and its affiliates, they will trust its content, never noticing the disinformation. An example of how this works happened with Rhianna and her fans. Rhianna accidentally shared a misleading tweet during the Australian fires and most of her followers spread the tweet without thinking twice about the content because they trust her, and this trust kept them from contemplating whether or not the content of her tweet was questionable. Knowing that Rhianna is capable of getting duped into sharing disinformation will not stop her followers from trusting her in the future, either. Even if Rhianna began to willingly share disinformation, many of her fans would still share it because they will always trust her. This is true for organizations like the SPN and populists like Trump. Many supporters of the SPN do not consider the validity of the SPN's content because they trust the network's values and goals. The ends justifies the means.
